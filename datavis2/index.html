The Causes and Impacts of Global Warming

What is Global Warming?

Global warming refers to the long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature due to human activities, particularly the release of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane, and nitrous oxide. These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, preventing it from escaping into space, a phenomenon known as the greenhouse effect. The primary sources of greenhouse gas emissions are the burning of fossil fuels for energy, deforestation, and industrial processes. As global temperatures rise, this leads to significant environmental impacts, including melting polar ice, rising sea levels, more frequent and severe extreme weather events, and disruptions to ecosystems and biodiversity.

The Causes of Global Warming
Greenhouse Gas Emissions by Sector (2020)

Global warming is primarily driven by human activities that release large quantities of greenhouse gases (GHGs) into the atmosphere. The burning of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas for energy and transportation is the largest contributor to GHG emissions, accounting for a significant portion of carbon dioxide (CO₂) and other heat-trapping gases. Industrial processes, deforestation, and agricultural activities further contribute to the rise in these gases. Deforestation reduces the Earth’s capacity to absorb CO₂, while agricultural practices release methane and nitrous oxide, both potent greenhouse gases.

The pie chart provides a breakdown of global greenhouse gas emissions by sector for the year 2020. As shown, the electricity and heat production sector is the largest emitter, contributing over 30% of total GHG emissions. Transportation follows closely behind, with significant contributions from industry, agriculture, and buildings. These sectors are responsible for the majority of human-induced global warming, as they collectively produce vast amounts of emissions that trap heat in the Earth’s atmosphere. By addressing emissions from these key sectors, particularly through renewable energy, more sustainable agriculture, and improved energy efficiency, the global community can work toward mitigating climate change.

CO₂ Emissions Distribution by Country (2022)

This map further highlights the regional disparities in CO₂ emissions per capita, showing how industrialized nations tend to have significantly higher emissions. Countries with large populations and extensive fossil fuel use, such as the United States and parts of the Middle East, emit substantially more CO₂ per person. In contrast, many developing countries contribute far less per capita, reflecting lower energy consumption and industrial output. This data underscores the importance of focusing emission reduction efforts on high-emission regions, while ensuring that global climate policies take into account the differing capacities and responsibilities of nations in tackling climate change.
